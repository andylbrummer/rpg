# World & Setting

## The Reach

A decaying continental shelf of city-states built on the bones of a collapsed empire. The empire didn't fall to war — it consumed itself. Its necromantic infrastructure, the system that powered everything from agriculture to governance, outlived the people who understood it. Now the machinery grinds on, half-broken, tended by factions who each claim to understand what the empire knew.

## Tone Touchstones

- **Abercrombie** — no heroes, just people with swords and bad decisions
- **Garth Nix (Old Kingdom)** — necromancy as structured system with deep rules, not just "raise skeleton"
- **Tamsyn Muir (Locked Tomb)** — bone and bone-magic as mundane infrastructure, dark humor amid genuine horror
- **This Is How You Lose the Time War** — poetic world-state, the sense that history is a contested landscape
- **Jeff Vandermeer** — blooms as zones of biological wrongness, nature running on broken instructions
- **Charles Stross (Laundry Files)** — bureaucratic paranoia, the institution you serve may already be compromised

## Key World Rules

### The Bone Tithe
The dead are owed to the infrastructure. Families negotiate which ancestors power what. This is politics, not horror. Wealthy families buy exemptions. Poor families have no choice. The tithe system is the backbone of civic order and the source of most resentment.

### The Engines
Ancient necromantic machines that keep cities livable — water purification, heating, defensive wards, agricultural animation. Nobody fully understands them. When they break, people die. Maintaining them is the single most important job in the Reach, and the single most politically contested.

### Magic Is Physical
It costs something visible. Blood, bone, years, memory. No mana bars — spell costs are narrative and mechanical. A Bonewarden who over-extends might lose a finger. An Inkblood who digs too deep into forbidden texts forgets the name of someone they loved.

### No Elder Evil
There's no dark lord. The antagonists are factions with incompatible visions for what the Reach should become. The real horror is that several of them might be right.

## The Blooms

Across the Reach, pockets of concentrated necromantic energy are appearing spontaneously — warping local fauna and flora into something other. Not undead exactly — more like life running on the wrong instructions.

- Bone coral growing from living trees
- Animals that don't die when they should, their bodies reshaping around the injury
- Areas where the dead get back up but aren't anyone's dead — no necromancer claimed them
- Geometry that feels wrong — rooms that are slightly larger inside, corridors that curve when they shouldn't

The blooms are accelerating. Nobody agrees on why. Each faction has a theory, and each theory implies a different course of action.

## Bloom Interaction

The blooms are the central crisis, not scenery. Players encounter bloom hearts — source nodes at the center of each bloom zone — and must choose how to respond. Each choice has mechanical and political consequences.

| Action | Effect | Requirements | Faction Implications |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Destroy** | Eliminates the bloom locally, area becomes inert | High combat difficulty (bloom heart fights back) | Stillness approves; Convocation hostile |
| **Contain** | Prevents spread, bloom persists but stabilized | Fieldwright + Bonewarden coordination (2+ turns on site) | Bureau approves; neutral to most |
| **Harvest** | Extract bloom energy/materials for use | Convocation-provided equipment or Heretic branch | Convocation approves; Stillness hostile; risk of mutation to harvesters |
| **Research** | Map and study without intervening | Cartography reputation + Inkblood in party | Cartography approves; time cost (1 turn), no immediate resolution |
| **Ignore** | Bloom continues spreading on the world clock | No cost | All factions react based on proximity to their interests |

Bloom hearts scale in difficulty with campaign progression. Early blooms are containable by a prepared party. Late-game blooms may require faction support or multi-expedition approaches.

The Complication roll can accelerate bloom timelines — under Bloom Siege, ignoring a heart has consequences within 2-3 turns rather than 5-6.

## Geography

The overworld is a scarred landscape — ruined imperial highways, contested mountain passes, dead forests that aren't quite dead. City-states cling to functioning Engines. The spaces between are increasingly wild.

Travel is dangerous. Time passes while you move. The world doesn't wait.
